BEB Fleet Sizing & Scheduling with Limited Overnight Charging

Branch‑and‑Price · Set Partitioning · SOC Network

This project focuses on fleet sizing and scheduling of Battery Electric Buses (BEBs) under real-world operational constraints. I developed a spatial–temporal–state-of-charge (SOC) network model that captures the interaction between battery dynamics, trip chaining, and limited overnight charging capacity . The problem was formulated as a single-depot vehicle scheduling problem and solved using an advanced Branch-and-Price algorithm, enabling scalability to large transit networks. Through a real-world case study on the Twin Cities transit system, the model optimized more than 1,000 scheduled trips across multiple routes and charging stations, balancing fleet costs, charging station capacity, and service reliability. Results demonstrated that accounting for limited charging infrastructure is critical; ignoring it can underestimate fleet needs and lead to infeasible schedules. The framework proved effective in generating cost-efficient electrification strategies, reducing unnecessary deadheading and idle time while ensuring buses maintain reliable state-of-charge levels. Scenario analysis revealed the operational impact of minimum SOC thresholds, charging restrictions during peak electricity hours, and non-dynamic scheduling policies, offering practical insights for transit agencies. Overall, this research provides a scalable decision-support tool that can guide agencies in planning BEB transitions, evaluate charging infrastructure investments, and reduce greenhouse gas emissions while ensuring reliable service delivery.

  • Inputs: GTFS trips/blocks, terminal transfer times, charger locations/rates, energy model.
  • Outputs: Duty roster, charging plan, fleet size, peak power, station utilization.
  • Methods: Column generation, strong branching, cut strengthening on infeasible patterns.

Incremental Electrification under Uncertain Energy Consumption

Two‑Stage Stochastic MILP · Benders

My research develops a two-stage stochastic mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) framework for the multi-phase electrification of urban bus networks under uncertain energy consumption. The first stage determines strategic decisions—such as selecting routes for electrification, siting and sizing charging stations, assigning routes to charging facilities, and determining fleet size—while accounting for stochastic charging demand and time-of-use electricity tariffs. The second stage optimizes daily charging operations for the battery electric bus fleet given realized operational conditions, ensuring schedule adherence and minimizing operational disruptions. To solve this computationally challenging problem, I designed a Benders decomposition algorithm enhanced with a branch-and-cut framework, enabling efficient handling of the large-scale stochastic formulation. A real-world case study on a major metropolitan bus network demonstrates the model’s ability to generate cost-effective fleet deployment strategies, guide charging infrastructure and grid connection planning, and maintain high service reliability during the transition phases. This work showcases how advanced optimization techniques can directly inform sustainable, data-driven transportation planning and operations.

  • First stage: Facility locations, Facility capacity, Fleet size.
  • Second stage: scenario‑specific assignments and charging operations.
